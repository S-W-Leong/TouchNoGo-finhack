## Problem Statement
The repo started from a generic `real-time AML + sanctions screener` idea, but the stronger direction is already visible in the current prototype and autoplan: TNG does not need another flat alert table. The open problem is what happens after risk signals fire but before money leaves. Analysts still have to assemble evidence, separate facts from guesses, choose `step-up` vs `hold` vs `escalate`, draft notes, and reason about policy tradeoffs under time pressure.

For this hackathon, the product should be framed as a pre-payout decision workspace for fraud and AML ops, not as a replacement for TNG's core controls and not as a post-incident reporting tool.

## What Makes This Cool
The "whoa" is not one more risk score.

The "whoa" is:

1. one live case that already groups wallet, device, beneficiary, watchlist, and policy evidence
2. separate specialist agents showing what each one checked
3. a human-readable action recommendation with confidence and policy lines
4. a replay lab that shows what happens if TNG tightens or loosens a rule before rollout

That is a much sharper story than "we built fraud detection." It says: "we compress analyst work, keep humans in control, and let TNG test policy safely before customers feel it."

## Constraints
1. This is a hackathon build, not a production rollout. Build time is roughly 22 real hours once Day 1 build mode starts.
2. Submission needs a public repo, live URL, pitch deck, and short demo video.
3. The event requires meaningful AI and purposeful multi-cloud usage, not a cosmetic second cloud.
4. The current product is still a static HTML prototype with seeded cases and replay scenarios, not a live backend.
5. The team is small. Anything that adds a second major product surface has to earn its keep immediately.
6. The data has to stay synthetic and Malaysian-context enough to feel real in the room.
7. The repo currently contains checked-in credential material in `.env`; that must not survive into a public submission or live demo workflow.

## Premises
1. The winning wedge is analyst leverage, not raw detection. Existing controls are inputs. The product sits above them.
2. Security & Fraud is still the right track, but the demo must clearly show intervention before payout or release, not only after-the-fact explanation.
3. The internal fraud or AML analyst is the primary user. The user-facing customer experience is only shown if it directly strengthens the pre-payout story.
4. Replay and backtesting stay in scope because they are the clearest "next-gen" differentiator in the repo today.
5. Human approval on high-risk actions is a feature, not a compromise. It makes the AI story believable.

## Recommended Approach
Choose **Approach B** with one hard narrative rule borrowed from Approach C:

Every hero case must end in a visible pre-payout intervention.

That means the product remains the internal `RiskOps Agent` workspace already described in the repo, but the demo and copy cannot stop at "AI investigated the case." The story has to land on "TNG stopped, delayed, or challenged a risky flow before release, and the human can explain why."

This is the safest way to win from your current position because:

1. the repo already points here
2. the UI already supports this story
3. it is strong on AI, technical implementation, and differentiation
4. it avoids blowing scope on a full consumer rescue product

## Judging-Criteria Fit
1. **AI & Intelligent Systems**
   - The AI story is not "LLM predicts fraud."
   - The AI story is: specialist agents assemble evidence, explain uncertainty, recommend next steps, draft the case note, and help replay policy changes.
   - Deterministic rules stay deterministic.

2. **Technical Implementation**
   - The product should show one seeded end-to-end path that keeps working after the happy path click.
   - Edge cases to be ready for: duplicate signals, weak evidence, stale watchlist data, malformed agent output, and false positives.

3. **Multi-Cloud Service Usage**
   - The cloud split needs a sentence judges can remember.
   - Example shape: one cloud hosts the app and seeded case path, the second cloud handles AI or replay workloads for a concrete service reason.

4. **Impact & Feasibility**
   - The real user is the analyst who has to decide before money moves.
   - The measurable impact story is faster triage, clearer justification, safer holds, and better policy tuning.
   - The demo still needs one sharp "whoa" moment, not only abstract relevance.

5. **Presentation & Teamwork**
   - The pitch should be brutally linear: what happened, what the AI checked, what the human should do, what policy change the replay suggests.
   - Good idea, bad deck loses. Good idea, confused UI also loses.

## CTO Questions
Ask these in this order. Stop when you get a strong answer. Do not spray all of them if the conversation is short.

1. If we build above your existing controls instead of replacing them, where is the biggest pain today: alert triage, evidence gathering, action approval, SAR drafting, or policy tuning?
2. For this hackathon, which would feel more real to you: a better analyst decision workspace, or a consumer-facing scam intervention right before payout?
3. Which single abuse pattern should we make the hero case if we want it to feel closest to TNG reality: dormant-wallet takeover, mule-ring collection, merchant QR abuse, near-watchlist cross-border payout, or onboarding synthetic identity?
4. When you hear "meaningful AI" in this setting, what do you want to see beyond summarization: evidence linking, action recommendation, policy reasoning, report drafting, or replay/backtesting?
5. What would make the multi-cloud split feel purposeful rather than cosmetic to you?
6. If the system recommends `hold`, `step-up`, or `escalate`, what evidence would you need to trust that recommendation in a real ops environment?
7. If we only earn one "whoa" moment in four minutes, should it be the live case investigation or the replay lab showing a policy tradeoff?
8. If this were piloted on Monday with fake data removed, what is the narrowest workflow you would actually test first?

## Success Criteria
1. In under 4 minutes, a judge can see one case move from signal to evidence to action to exported note.
2. The AI story is clear: separate agents, grounded evidence, explicit uncertainty, human approval.
3. The track fit is clear: the system helps stop or challenge risky money movement before release.
4. The multi-cloud split is easy to explain in one sentence and one diagram.
5. One replay scenario shows a real tradeoff: more bad payouts stopped versus more normal users delayed.
6. The repo is public-safe, easy to run, and does not leak credentials.
